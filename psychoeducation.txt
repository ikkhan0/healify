Depression 
Depression is more than just feeling sad or having a bad day. It is a common and serious medical condition that affects 
how you think, feel, and act. It can make everyday life feel heavy and hopeless, but the good news is that it is 
treatable. Many people recover fully with the right support.  
Depression happens when feelings of sadness, emptiness, or loss of interest last for weeks or months and interfere 
with work, school, relationships, or daily tasks. It is not a sign of weakness or laziness. Many factors can lead to 
depression like family history, stressful life events (like job loss, death of a loved one, financial problems, or 
relationship issues), physical illness, negative thinking, rumination, or even lack of social support. Biochemical 
changes in brain can also lead to mood issues. 
 In Pakistan, cultural pressures, economic challenges, and stigma can make it harder to recognize or talk about. But 
depression is real, and seeking help is a sign of strength. It is not something you can “just snap out of.” With time and 
proper care, most people feel much better. 
Some statistics 
Depression affects millions of people worldwide. According to the World Health Organization (WHO) in 2025, about 
332 million people globally live with depression. Roughly 5.7% of adults experience it in any given year— 4.6% of 
men and 6.9% of women. Women are about 1.5 times more likely to have depression than men. It is also common in 
older adults (5.9% of those aged 70 and above) and more than 10% of pregnant women or new mothers worldwide. 
In Pakistan, exact national figures are limited, but studies show depression is very common, especially among young 
adults (rates reported between 22% and 64% in some urban areas). Postpartum depression (depression after birth of 
the child) affects many new mothers here too. Stigma and limited services make many cases go unreported. The good 
news? Awareness is growing, and help is available. Depression does not discriminate—it can affect anyone, rich or 
poor, young or old. 
Signs and Symptoms 
Depression shows up in different ways. Doctors use clear guidelines (like the DSM-5. ICD-11): if you have five or 
more  of the following symptoms for at least two weeks, and they are a change from how you usually feel, it may be 
depression. At least one symptom must be depressed mood or loss of interest. 
Emotional signs: 
•     Feeling sad, empty, hopeless, or tearful most of the day, nearly every day. 
•     Loss of interest or pleasure in things you once enjoyed (hobbies, family time, food, sex). 
•     Irritability, anger, or frustration over small things. 
•     Feelings of worthlessness, guilt, or excessive self-blame. 
Physical signs: 
•     Big changes in appetite or weight (loss or gain without trying). 
•     Trouble sleeping (insomnia) or sleeping too much. 
•     Feeling tired or having no energy, even for small tasks. 
•     Slowed movements or speech, or feeling restless and unable to sit still (others may notice this). 
Thinking and behaviour signs: 
•     Difficulty concentrating, making decisions, or remembering things. 
•     Recurrent thoughts of death, dying, or suicide.

Symptoms can vary. Some people feel more irritable than sad; children or teens may seem angry or withdraw from 
friends. In Pakistan, people sometimes describe it as “tension,” “dil ka gham,” or constant worry. Physical complaints 
like headaches, body aches, or stomach problems are common too. If these symptoms last and affect your life, it is not 
“normal sadness”—it is time to pay attention. 
Self-Care and Management: Small Steps That Help 
You do not have to wait for professional help to start feeling better. Self-care can reduce symptoms and support 
recovery. These strategies are safe and effective for mild to moderate depression. Use them alongside other treatment 
if needed. 
1.   Stay connected: Talk to a trusted family member, friend, or neighbour. Share how you feel—even a short call 
can reduce loneliness. In Pakistani culture, family support is strong; lean on it without shame. 
2.   Move your body: Exercise is one of the best natural mood boosters. Walk for 30 minutes a day (or even 10 
minutes a few times). It releases feel-good chemicals in the brain. Start small— no gym needed. 
3.   Eat and drink well: Have regular, balanced meals with fruits, vegetables, daal, roti, and proteins. Stay 
hydrated. Cut down on too much tea, sugary drinks, or junk food. Good nutrition helps your brain work better. 
4.   Sleep better: Go to bed and wake up at the same time. Avoid screens (phone, TV) one hour before bed. A 
dark, quiet room helps. Poor sleep makes depression worse. 
5.   Do relaxing activities: Try deep breathing, prayer, listening to Quran/religious scriptures, listening to music, 
gardening, or spending time in nature. Mindfulness or simple meditation (in Urdu if possible) can calm racing 
thoughts. 
6.   Set small goals: Break tasks into tiny steps. Make your bed, wash one dish, or walk around the block/colony. 
Celebrate every win. This builds confidence. 
7.   Limit alcohol and avoid drugs: They may seem to help at first but make depression worse later. 
8.   Track your mood: Write in a simple notebook what you did each day and how you felt. Patterns will show 
what helps you to feel better. 
9.   Peer support: Join a community group, mosque circle, or online forum (carefully). Knowing that you are not 
alone helps. 
Self-care is not a quick fix but doing even one or two things daily can make a big difference over weeks. Be patient 
with yourself— recovery takes time. 
When and How to Seek Professional Help 
Self-care is great, but seek professional help if: 
•     Symptoms last more than two weeks and affect work, studies, or relationships. 
•     You have thoughts of harming yourself or suicide (call for help immediately). 
•     You feel hopeless and nothing improves. 
•     Physical symptoms (pain, weight loss) appear without clear cause. 
Professional treatment works well for most people. Options include: 
•     Talking therapy (counselling or CBT— cognitive behavioural therapy): Helps change negative thinking 
patterns. 
•     Medication: Antidepressants (prescribed by a doctor) balance brain chemicals. They are not addictive and are 
safe when taken as directed. 
•     Combination: Therapy + medicine often works best.

Anxiety 
Anxiety is a normal human response to stress or perceived threat. It helps us stay alert and respond to danger (like the 
"fight-or-flight" response). Everyone feels anxious sometimes —  before an exam, a job interview, or an important 
event. 
Anxiety becomes a problem (an anxiety disorder) when it is excessive, persistent, difficult to control, and interferes 
with daily life —  work, studies, relationships, or sleep and sometimes appear as panic attacks. It is one of the most 
common mental health conditions worldwide. Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is a frequent form, involving 
ongoing worry about many everyday things. Other common anxiety-related issues are social anxiety, phobias, 
separation anxiety, selective mutism, and worry linked to trauma. Anxiety often overlaps with depression or sleep 
problems. 
Signs and Symptoms of Anxiety (especially Generalized Anxiety) 
To meet criteria for Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD), a person typically has: 
•     Excessive anxiety and worry about a number of events or activities (e.g., work, health, family, minor matters), 
occurring more days than not for at least 6 months. 
•     Difficulty controlling the worry. 
•     The worry causes significant distress or impairs functioning. 
It is usually accompanied by at least 3 of the following symptoms (only 1 needed in children): 
Physical Symptoms: 
•     Restlessness or feeling keyed up/on edge 
•     Being easily fatigued/tired 
•     Muscle tension (e.g., tight shoulders, headaches) 
•     Sleep disturbance (trouble falling or staying asleep, restless sleep) 
•     Sweating, trembling, rapid heartbeat, shortness of breath, nausea, or stomach upset (can occur during 
heightened worry or panic-like moments) 
Cognitive/Emotional Symptoms: 
•     Difficulty concentrating or mind going blank 
•     Irritability 
•     Overthinking plans and worst-case scenarios 
•     Feeling unable to relax 
•     Indecisiveness or fear of making the wrong decision 
Common thoughts include: “What if something bad happens?”, “I can’t handle this”, or constant scanning for 
problems. 
Symptoms vary from person to person. Some feel more physical (racing heart, stomach issues), others more mental 
(racing worries). Anxiety can also cause avoidance of situations that trigger worry. 
When such symptoms get associated with encountering certain objects (animals, injections/needles, water, etc.) and 
situations (height, presenting work, separating from caregiver/loved one, etc.) then anxiety may be depicted as 
phobias, social anxiety, separation anxiety, agoraphobia, etc.

What Keeps Anxiety Going? (The Vicious Cycle) 
•     A small worry or bodily sensation starts. 
•     You interpret it as dangerous or threatening. 
•     Anxiety increases → more symptoms appear. 
•     You may avoid situations, seek constant reassurance, or overthink, which temporarily relieves anxiety but 
makes it stronger long-term. 
Understanding this cycle helps break it. 
Interventions and Coping Strategies 
Good news: Anxiety is highly treatable. Most people improve significantly with the right help. Many recover fully or 
learn to manage it well so it no longer controls their life. 
1. Self-Help Strategies You Can Start Today 
•    Breathing exercises: Regular deep breathing may help reduce tension and encourage relaxation, according to 
studies. Stress is your body's reaction to a perceived or actual threat or danger. Stress hormones, also referred 
to as the "fight-or-flight" response, are released by your body to prepare you for an emergency. You may 
experience feelings of overwhelm, agitation, or panic during the fight-or-flight reaction. Your respiration 
quickens, your heart races, and your muscles tighten. When you are under stress, it is typical for your 
breathing to become shallow and rapid. However, you may relax by using the diaphragm, the muscle under 
your ribcage, to take deep, leisurely breaths. Depp breathing helps: 
o   Reducing stress 
o   Lowering heart rate 
o   Improving sleep quality 
o   Reduce brain fog and improves concentration and attention 
•    Grounding techniques: This is a first-line coping mechanism for the acute physical symptoms of panic, like 
dizziness, hyperventilation, or a fast heartbeat. It is frequently used to control overthinking, lessen racing 
thoughts, and break anxiety cycles. It serves as a kind of mindfulness practice that anchors a person in the 
present moment by using the five senses to divert attention from overpowering. It is utilized to provide 
immediate stabilization during periods of extreme distress. Acute fear reactions brought on by phobic stimuli 
can be controlled with this strategy.  
•     Progressive Muscle Relaxation: Progressively tensing (5-10 sec.) and then releasing/relaxing (5-10 sec.) 
muscle groups from toes to head. It helps in relaxing body and release stress and tension in muscles that is 
common in anxiety while encountering stressful situation and thoughts. This can be done daily at any time that 
suits you. It can be done in standing, sitting, and lying position, whatever suits you. 
•     Challenge worries: Ask yourself: 
“What is the evidence for and against this worry?” 
“What would I tell a friend in this situation?” 
“What is the realistic outcome, not the worst-case?” 
             Carefully analysing situation help to address the problem logically. Journalling or writing diary to note down 
experiences after experiencing anxiety provoking situation can help to understand your reaction pattern and plan to 
address it effectively in future.

•     Lifestyle basics: Small changes in lifestyle helps. Regular exercise (like walking), consistent sleep routine, 
balanced meals, limited caffeine and alcohol, schedule “worry time” (set aside 15–20 minutes daily to write 
worries, then move on), time management, using organizers to prioritise and manage daily activities, can help. 
•     Stay active and connected: Avoid complete avoidance of feared situations —  gentle facing builds 
confidence. Talk to trusted people. 
•     Mindfulness: Being mindful and aware of the current moment is the condition of mindfulness. You 
concentrate on your thoughts, feelings, body, and environment during this. You act in this way without passing 
judgment. All that exists is consciousness of the current moment. Mindfulness can be practiced in a variety of 
ways. When performing routine actions like eating or cleaning your teeth, you can practice mindfulness. Or 
you can set aside a regular time to practice mindfulness. You can do it in a group or by yourself. The nervous 
system is calmed by mindfulness. Cortisol, the body's stress hormone, is lowered as a result. By assisting you 
in maintaining your attention in the here and now rather than becoming trapped in worry loops, where you 
repeatedly think about the same subject, mindfulness can help reduce anxiety. By altering the way the brain 
interprets pain signals, mindfulness can alleviate chronic pain.  
2. Professional Treatments (Evidence-Based) 
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is the gold-standard psychological treatment for anxiety. It helps you: 
•     Understand and change unhelpful thinking patterns. 
•     Gradually face fears instead of avoiding them. 
•     Learn practical skills to manage symptoms. 
CBT is usually short-term (8–16 sessions) and highly effective. Other helpful approaches include mindfulness-based 
therapies or exposure therapy for specific fears. 
A combination of CBT + medication can be especially helpful for moderate to severe anxiety. Treatment is usually 
continued for 6–12 months after improvement to prevent relapse. 
When to Seek Professional Help and Referrals 
Seek help if anxiety: 
•     Lasts more than a few weeks and feels overwhelming. 
•     Interferes with work, studies, relationships, or daily activities. 
•     Causes avoidance of important situations. 
•     Is accompanied by depression, panic attacks, substance use, or thoughts of self-harm.

Panic Attacks 
A panic attack is a sudden, intense surge of fear or discomfort that reaches a peak within minutes. It feels 
overwhelming and can happen even when there is no real danger. Many people describe it as one of the scariest 
experiences they've had— like a "false alarm" in the body's fight-or-flight system. 
Panic attacks are common manifestation of intense level of anxiety and fear. About 1 in 3 people may experience at 
least one in their lifetime, but not everyone develops a problem from them. 
Signs and Symptoms 
A panic attack typically involves 4 or more of the following symptoms (based on DSM-5 criteria) that start abruptly 
and peak quickly:

Physical Symptoms (most common): 
•     Pounding or racing heart (palpitations) 
•     Sweating 
•     Trembling or shaking 
•     Shortness of breath or smothering sensation 
•     Feeling of choking 
•     Chest pain or discomfort 
•     Nausea or abdominal distress 
•     Dizziness, lightheadedness, or feeling faint 
•     Chills or hot flushes 
•     Numbness or tingling (paresthesias) 
Cognitive/Emotional Symptoms: 
•     Feelings of unreality (derealization) or detachment from yourself (depersonalization) 
•     Fear of losing control or "going crazy" 
•     Fear of dying or impending doom 
Attacks usually last 5–20 minutes (though the fear afterward can linger). Some people also have nocturnal panic 
attacks that wake them from sleep. 
Some can have panic disorder that involve experiencing recurrent, unexpected panic attacks plus at least one month 
of: 
•     Persistent worry about having more attacks or what they might mean (e.g., fear of having a heart attack, losing 
control, or "going crazy"). 
•     Significant changes in behavior to avoid situations where attacks might happen (this can lead to agoraphobia 
in some cases). 
Panic attacks can also occur in other anxiety conditions, but in panic disorder they often come "out of the blue." The 
good news: it is highly treatable, and many people recover fully or manage it well. 
 
Important: These symptoms can mimic serious medical issues (e.g., heart attack). Always see a doctor first to rule out 
physical causes like thyroid problems, heart conditions, or asthma. 
What Keeps Panic Going? (The Vicious Cycle) 
1.   A bodily sensation (e.g., faster heartbeat) occurs. 
2.   You interpret it as dangerous ("I'm having a heart attack!"). 
3.   Anxiety skyrockets → more physical symptoms. 
4.   Fear of future attacks leads to hypervigilance (constantly scanning your body) or avoidance (e.g., avoiding 
crowds, exercise, or leaving home). 
5.   This avoidance and worry actually maintain the problem. 
Understanding this cycle is a key part of recovery— symptoms are uncomfortable but not dangerous.

Self-Help Steps You Can Start Today: 
•     Breathe properly: During an attack, try slow diaphragmatic breathing (belly breathing). Inhale slowly for 4 
counts through your nose, hold for 2, exhale for 6 through your mouth. This counters hyperventilation. Avoid 
over-breathing. 
•     Ground ing techniques: It helps to distract and stay in present. 
•     Ride it out: Remind yourself: "This is a panic attack. It will peak and pass. I'm safe. These sensations are not 
harmful." Most attacks end within minutes even without doing anything. 
•     Stay active: Regular exercise, good sleep, limiting caffeine/alcohol, and healthy routines help reduce overall 
anxiety. 
•     Track your attacks: Note triggers, symptoms, and what helps through journaling and maintaining diary. This 
reduces fear through knowledge and avoid triggers timely. 
•     Challenge fearful thoughts: Ask: "What's the evidence this is dangerous? Have I survived these before?" 
Challenging thought that trigger panic helps to stay calm. 
When to Seek Help: 
•     If attacks are frequent, interfere with work/relationships, or you start avoiding important activities. 
•     If you have thoughts of harming yourself. 
•     Start with a GP/primary doctor for medical check-up, then a mental health professional (psychologist, 
psychiatrist, or counselor) experienced in anxiety. 
 
Trauma  
Trauma refers to an extremely stressful or terrifying event (or series of events) that threatens your life, safety, or the 
safety of others. Common examples include: 
•     Serious accidents (e.g., car crashes) 
•     Natural disasters (floods, earthquakes) 
•     Physical or sexual assault 
•     Combat or violence (like witnessing war) 
•     Sudden death or injury of a loved one 
•     Witnessing harm to others 
Not everyone who experiences trauma develops long-term problems. Many people have temporary reactions— like 
shock, fear, sadness, trouble sleeping, or replaying the event in their mind—that naturally improve over days or weeks. 
These are normal reactions to abnormal events. 
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that can develop when trauma symptoms 
persist, intensify, or interfere with daily life (work, relationships, sleep, etc.) for more than one month. Symptoms 
usually start within 3 months of the event but can appear later. PTSD is not a sign of weakness— it is a real, treatable 
response in the brain and body to overwhelming stress. 
About 6 out of every 100 people will experience PTSD in their lifetime. Women are more likely than men to develop 
it, and risk can increase with factors like the severity of the trauma, lack of support, or previous experiences. 
Signs and Symptoms of PTSD 
PTSD symptoms are grouped into four main categories (based on DSM-5 criteria). To be diagnosed, a person 
typically needs symptoms from each category that last more than a month and cause significant distress.

1.   Re-experiencing / Intrusion Symptoms (at least 1 required) 
o    Flashbacks (feeling or acting as if the trauma is happening again) 
o    Nightmares or distressing dreams about the event 
o    Unwanted, recurring memories or thoughts 
o    Intense emotional or physical reactions (e.g., heart racing, sweating) when reminded of the trauma 
2.   Avoidance Symptoms (at least 1 required) 
o    Avoiding thoughts, feelings, or conversations about the trauma 
o    Avoiding people, places, activities, objects, or situations that remind you of it (e.g., avoiding driving 
after a car accident) 
3.   Negative Changes in Cognition and Mood (at least 2 required) 
o    Trouble remembering important parts of the event 
o    Persistent negative beliefs (e.g., “I am bad,” “The world is completely dangerous,” “No one can be 
trusted”) 
o    Blaming yourself or others excessively for the event 
o    Persistent negative emotions (fear, horror, anger, guilt, shame) 
o    Loss of interest in activities you used to enjoy 
o    Feeling detached, estranged, or emotionally numb 
o    Inability to feel positive emotions (happiness, love) 
4.   Changes in Arousal and Reactivity (at least 2 required) 
o    Feeling irritable, angry outbursts, or aggressive behavior 
o    Reckless or self-destructive behavior 
o    Being overly alert (“on guard” or hypervigilant) 
o    Exaggerated startle response (easily startled) 
o    Difficulty concentrating 
o    Trouble falling or staying asleep 
Other common reactions (not required for diagnosis but often present): 
•     Shock, disbelief, helplessness 
•     Physical symptoms (headaches, stomach issues, rapid heartbeat) 
•     Increased use of alcohol or drugs 
•     Relationship strain or social withdrawal 
Symptoms can vary in intensity and may come and go. They can worsen with reminders of the trauma or added stress. 
Interventions and Coping Strategies 
Good news is PTSD is highly treatable. Most people improve significantly with help. Early support can prevent 
symptoms from becoming chronic. 
First Step – Safety

When a person is experiencing trauma based symptoms, the natural response of nervous system stays on high alert and 
it’s the protecting mechanism of brain, just to help the individual to survive. The primary step towards healing is 
helping the person to feel safe again. It’s not just about being physically safe., It’s also about feeling secure, knowing 
that he/she isn’t alone and having better ways to cope with the overwhelming situations. Now, the question is, how to 
attain a sense of safety, here are some quick ways:  
1.   Change Physical Space  
If a person is in physically or emotionally threatening place, it is better to step out of that situation for a while, 
especially if feeling overwhelmed (This isn’t the solution to the core problem, just a way to have some room for the 
nervous system to breathe). This means,  
-     Going to a different room 
-     Stepping outside 
-     Moving away from the trigger 
 
2.   Connect with Trustworthy Individual   
Reaching out to someone you trust can signal to your nervous system that you're not facing the threat by yourself, a 
small brief interaction with a trustworthy individual helps in regulation. It can include calling/texting a friend or sit 
with a family member.  If no one is available right now, try imagining someone safe or trustworthy loved one, a 
mentor, or even a comforting memory of a person. 
3.   Connecting with Present Moment  
Grounding techniques brings us back to the present moment, to the “NOW”.  It activates sensory system and gently 
pulls brain out of the trauma response.  
 
4.   Butterfly Hug – Self Soothing   
This technique is gentle and highly effective for calming the nervous system. It sends a signal of safety through 
bilateral stimulation.  
How to do it: 
1. Cross your arms over your chest so that each hand rests on the opposite arm (near your shoulders or collarbone) 
2. Close your eyes if it feels comfortable 
3. Tap slowly, alternately—left, right, left, right—like the gentle flutter of a butterfly's wings 
4. As you tap, imagine a safe place or simply breathe naturally 
5. Continue for 1–2 minutes, or as long as feels right 
This technique was developed by EMDR practitioners Lucina Artigas and Ignacio Jarero in 2014 as a self-
administered tool for trauma survivors.  
There are many resources and techniques available to help calm the mind and body. However, if the symptoms feel 
overwhelming, reaching out for professional support is always a wise choice.

•     Practice relaxation: Deep breathing and progressive muscle relaxation help when experiencing symptoms.

Drug Addiction or Substance Use 
Substance use means taking drugs, alcohol, or other chemicals that change how you feel, think, or act. It 
becomes a problem —  called Substance Use Disorder (SUD) or drug addiction —  when it starts controlling 
your life, harming your health, relationships, work, or studies, even though you know it is causing damage. 
It is not a moral failing or “bad character.” It is a medical condition caused by a mix of things: easy 
availability of drugs, peer pressure, stress from money problems or family issues, curiosity, or underlying 
mental health challenges like depression. Many people in Pakistan start with cannabis (charas) or prescribed 
medicine (like pain killers) and move to stronger substances like heroin. Substance use is treatable. With 
support, most people can recover and live healthy lives.  
Drug Addiction: Some Statistics 
Globally, drug use is at a record high. According to the UNODC World Drug Report 2025, about 316 million 
people (6% of those aged 15–64) used drugs (excluding alcohol and tobacco) in 2023. Around 64 million 
people worldwide have drug use disorders. Only 1 in 12 gets any treatment. Cannabis (like chars) is the 
most used, followed by opioids, amphetamines, and cocaine. Drug use causes nearly half a million deaths 
yearly and millions of lost healthy years of life. 
In Pakistan, the problem is serious and growing. The latest national data shows about 6.7 million people 
(roughly 6% of adults aged 15–64) use illicit drugs. Of these, 4.25 million suffer from drug use disorders 
and need structured help. Cannabis (chars) is the most common (about 3.6% of the population), followed by 
opiates like heroin and opium (around 1%). Poly-drug use (mixing substances) affects 20% of users. 
Substance use costs families, communities, and the country in lost productivity, health problems, and crime. 
Signs and Symptoms

Doctors use clear guidelines (DSM-5 and ICD-11) to identify substance use disorder. If someone shows 2 or 
more  of the following in the past 12 months, it may be a problem. Severity is mild (2–3 signs), moderate (4–
5), or severe (6 or more). 
Impaired control: 
• 
Using drug more than planned or for longer than intended. 
• 
Strong cravings or urges to use. 
• 
Repeated unsuccessful attempts to cut down or stop. 
• 
Spending a lot of time getting, using, or recovering from the substance. 
Social and work problems: 
• 
Failing at work, school, or home responsibilities because of use. 
• 
Continuing use even when it causes fights with family or friends. 
• 
Giving up important hobbies, sports, or family time because of the substance. 
Risky use: 
• 
Using in dangerous situations (e.g., driving, operating machinery). 
• 
Continuing use despite knowing it is causing physical or mental harm. 
Physical dependence (for some substances): 
• 
Needing more of the substance to get the same effect (tolerance). 
• 
Withdrawal symptoms when stopping (shakes, anxiety, nausea, pain). 
Other common signs in daily life: sudden weight loss or gain, bloodshot eyes, neglected appearance, mood 
swings, lying about whereabouts, money problems, or legal issues. In Pakistan, people may complain of 
“body pain” or “sleeplessness” instead of admitting drug use. Families often notice secrecy, isolation, or 
stealing. If these last and affect life, it is time to act— early help prevents severe addiction.

You do not need to wait for a doctor to start improving. Self-care works best for mild problems or alongside 
professional treatment. These steps are safe and practical for Pakistan. 
1.   Acknowledge the problem: Be honest with yourself. Write down how the substance is harming you 
and your family. This builds motivation. Compassion is the primary indicator for a change, any 
positive change, so self-compassion is so important that we need to understand what's happening 
rather than criticizing that urge or craving or being judgmental about it.  
2.   Grounding Yourself  
Whenever a craving and urge hits, the brain is in the loop, and we need to interrupt it. One way indulging in 
grounding techniques.  It helps in connecting us to the present moment and to our surroundings. 
Another quick way to ground ourselves is cold exposure, for example splash cold water on our face. We can 
hold ice cubes and put our hands or feet in the cold water for a few minutes so that it can activate the 
nervous system and so that it can help the nervous system to regulate and break the thought cycle to use 
drug.

Self-care helps, but get professional help if: 
• 
You cannot stop despite trying. 
• 
Withdrawal is severe (shaking, vomiting, severe anxiety).

Suicide 
Suicide is defined as death caused by self-directed injurious behavior with intent to die because of the 
behavior. Suicidal behavior can include suicidal ideation, suicide attempts, preparatory behavior, or rehearsal 
behavior.  
Suicide is a very sensitive topic. Every year, one million people die because of suicide. Suicide was the 
second leading cause of death among individuals between the ages of 10-14 and 25-34 according to Centers 
for Disease Control and Prevention. There are many reasons why someone might think about suicide. People 
may feel so overwhelmed that they cannot handle their problems, and they may lose interest, meaning, or 
purpose in life. This can lead them to take such actions. Research shows that a lack of support is a major 
reason for suicide. 
Signs of Suicidal Behaviors  
Verbal Signs 
Verbal signs of suicide include talking about ending life —  for example, "Sometimes I feel like I just want to 
die." A person may also talk about feeling guilty or having committed a sin. They may talk about feeling 
hopeless or having no reason to live. They may talk about feeling trapped or in unbearable pain or talk about 
being a burden to others. 
Behavioral Signs 
Behavioral signs can include suddenly starting alcohol use or drinking more alcohol than usual or using 
other substances. A person may become restless, agitated (getting angry easily), or anxious (very worried or 
fearful). They may withdraw or feel isolated —  outgoing or social people may interact with family or friends 
that decrease or stops completely. Preparatory behaviors may include giving away belongings or collecting 
things to inflict self-harm like medicines or pesticides. Rehearsal behavior involves practicing a suicide 
attempt without initiating the attempt.

There are many ways we can help someone who is thinking about hurting themselves. One of the best ways 
is to be there for them and offer support with kindness. It helps them to check in on them from time to time 
and helps them connect with others. We should not blame or question their thoughts but instead show 
understanding and care. If someone is in a very emotional state and thinking about self-harm, it is very 
important to be there for them. The most important thing is their safety.  
How to Help? 
First and foremost, try to help the client to express their suicidal thoughts freely by offering  
•    unconditional positive regard,  
•    being a good listener,  
•    being non-judgmental,  
•    being supportive,  
•    showing empathy,  
•    reflecting feelings or thoughts, and  
•    using open-ended questions.  
DO NOT LEAVE THE INDIVIDUAL ALONE.    
If it is urgent, we should help them get the right care or contact emergency services. If someone is having 
suicidal thoughts, it is important for them to reach out to a suicide prevention helpline or a professional who 
can help.
