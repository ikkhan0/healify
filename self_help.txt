Relaxation Techniques 
Deep Breathing 
         The practice of deep breathing, also called diaphragmatic breathing, is predicated on the idea 
that relaxation results from the integration of the mind and body. In this technique a person must 
slowly inhale and exhale while contracting the diaphragm. Regular deep breathing may help reduce 
tension and encourage relaxation. Stress is your body's reaction to a perceived or actual threat or 
danger. Stress hormones, also referred to as the "fight-or-flight" response, are released by your body 
to prepare you for an emergency. You may experience feelings of overwhelm, agitation, or panic 
during the fight-or-flight reaction. Your respiration quickens, your heart races, and your muscles 
tighten. When you are under stress, it is typical for your breathing to become shallow and rapid. 
However, you may relax by using the diaphragm, the muscle under your ribcage, to take deep, 
leisurely breaths.  According to researches, regular yoga practice that includes deep breathing is 
associated with increased levels of melatonin, a hormone that promotes relaxation and sleep. Deep 
breathing at or near bedtime may be a calming method to fall asleep deeply and restoratively if you 
suffer from insomnia. 
Is it safe to breathe deeply?  
 
Yes, deep breathing is quite safe for the majority of people. However:  
• Consult your healthcare provider if you have a serious medical condition, a cardiac condition, 
breathing issues or are pregnant. 
 • If deep breathing exercises cause unanticipated or worrisome symptoms like discomfort, dyspnea, 
or lightheadedness, cease immediately and seek medical attention. 
Deep Breathing Exercises  
Start with a realistic goal if you're new to deep breathing exercises. For a week, you may, for 
instance, do two minutes right before bed to observe how it feels. Locate a peaceful, cozy area where 
you won't be bothered. Some of the types of deep breathing exercises are as follows. 
1. Box breathing  
        Box breathing is one easy deep breathing method. The exercise's four phases form a box, hence 
its name. Sit or stand with your back straight to start box breathing. You can also lie down with your 
arms by your sides if that's more your style. Select the most comfortable posture for you. If you feel 
comfortable doing so, close your eyes. Take a moment to allow your body to settle, let go of any 
tightness in your jaw, and relax your shoulders. Next, take the actions listed below. 
1. Take a leisurely deep breath, four-count breath in (or see clock and take deep long breath for 4 
sec.). Put your hands on your stomach and feel it swell like a balloon if you'd like.  
2. Hold your breath for an additional four counts (4 sec.) after inhaling.  
3. Exhale consistently and gently through your mouth while mentally counting to four once more (or 
4 sec.).

4. Hold your breath for an additional four counts (or 4 sec.) after exhaling.  
5. Return to Step 1 and continue doing this for as long as you'd like. 
 
2. The 4-7-8 breathing method  
 
        This method is based on pranayama, an old yogic breathing technique. Sit comfortably with 
your back straight to start 4-7-8 breathing. Alternatively, you could lie down if you're utilizing this 
activity to help you go to sleep.  Your tongue should gently rest against the roof of your mouth, 
directly behind your upper front teeth, for the entire session. Relax your shoulders and jaws. When 
you're prepared, take the actions listed below. 
1. Inhale softly through your nose for four counts (or 4 sec.) .  
2. Hold your breath for seven counts (or 7 sec.) after inhaling.  
3. Make a "whoosh" sound as you forcefully exhale through your mouth for eight counts (8 sec.). 
Pursing your lips could make this easier.  
4. Return to Step 1 and continue the procedure up to three more times, for a total of four rounds.  
You may initially feel a little dizzy if you're a beginner. This is typical. You should only practice four 
rounds at a time at first. You can perform the exercise up to eight times as you become accustomed to 
it. 
 
3.   Breathe through alternate nostrils  
          Alternate nostril breathing is based on yogic breathing techniques, just like 4-7-8 breathing. 
You don't have to practice yoga in order to try it. Breathing through alternate nostrils can be a 
discipline in and of itself. According to research, you can reduce your stress levels by regularly 
practicing alternate nostril breathing for thirty minutes each day.  Sit comfortably on the floor or in a 
chair, keeping your back straight yet relaxed, to start alternate nostril breathing. Next, take the 
actions listed below. Close your right nostril by lightly pressing on it with your right thumb.  
1. Inhale deeply and slowly through your left nostril.  
2. Release your thumb from your right nostril and use your right ring finger (the one adjacent to your 
little finger) to close your left nostril. Slowly exhale through your right nostril.  
3. Inhale through your right nostril while keeping your left closed. Next, shut this nostril once more.  
4. Breathe out via your left nostril after opening it.  
5. Return to Step 1 and continue doing this for as long as you'd like. 
 
Progressive Muscle Relaxation 
Stress hormones during stressful situations and constant state of alert in anxiety and trauma-
based symptoms increase muscle tone. Body feels tense and muscles get strained.   
Progressive Muscle Relaxation helps to relax body and attain comfortable state of mind and 
feel good. 
Practicing Progressive Muscle Relaxation

Sit or stand with your back straight to start. You can also lie down with your arms by your 
sides if that's more your style. Select the most comfortable posture for you. Take a moment to 
allow your body to settle, let go of any tightness in your jaw, and relax your shoulders. This 
involves tensing and then releasing muscle groups from toes to head. It goes like: 
•    Tightly tense or squeeze your forehead muscles for count of 10 (or 10 sec.) to the 
extent that you feel uncomfortable. Then relax your forehead muscles for count of 10 
(or 10 sec.) to the extent that you feel relaxed. 
•    Tightly blow up your cheeks or squeeze your face muscles for count of 10 (or 10 sec.) 
to the extent that you feel uncomfortable. Then relax your forehead muscles for count 
of 10 (or 10 sec.) to the extent that you feel relaxed. 
•    Tightly close your eyes for count of 10 (or 10 sec.) to the extent that you feel 
uncomfortable. Then relax your forehead muscles for count of 10 (or 10 sec.) to the 
extent that you feel relaxed. 
•    Tightly stretch your neck for count of 10 (or 10 sec.) to the extent that you feel 
uncomfortable. Then relax your forehead muscles for count of 10 (or 10 sec.) to the 
extent that you feel relaxed. 
•    So on and so forth. Progressively tighten each part of your body like stretching arms; 
making fist; squeezing shoulders, belly, hips; stretching legs; folding toes; etc. then 
relax each. 
This acts as massage for body and get relaxed. It reduces muscle tone and increases blood 
circulation. 
Benefits 
Relaxation, including deep breathing and progressive muscle relaxation helps in reducing: 
•    Anxiety: Deep breathing is an effective way to lessen feelings of state anxiety, generalized 
anxiety, and situational anxiety. 
•    Panic Attack: By preventing hyperventilation, a typical sign, deep breathing helps control 
panic attacks.  
•    Depression: By enhancing autonomic balance and lowering cortisol levels, regular deep 
breathing can dramatically lower depression scores. It increases oxygen level in brain that 
helps to activate high order thinking areas and think rationally. 
•    Chronic Stress: By lowering autonomic activity and altering the balance in favor of 
parasympathetic dominance, breathwork is linked to decreased levels of stress.  
•    Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Deep breathing helps alleviate PTSD symptoms 
as activation of rational areas of brain help to discern memory based arousal from present 
non-threatening situation. 
•    Sleep problems and insomnia: Deep breathing can help improve the quality of sleep, 
especially when used to wind down. It also frequently lowers sleep latency.

Mindfulness 
 
          Being mindful and aware of the current moment is the condition of mindfulness. Your aim 
would be to concentrate on your current thoughts, feelings, body, and environment without any 
judgment as good or bad. Focus is consciousness of the current moment. Mindfulness is beneficial to 
almost everyone. It can be practiced in a variety of ways when performing routine actions like eating 
or cleaning your teeth. Or you can set aside a regular time to practice mindfulness. You can do it in a 
group or by yourself. Breathing techniques, guided imagery, and other techniques to calm the body 
and mind can be used in mindfulness meditation.  
 
Some Examples of Mindfulness Exercises 
 
           Do simple activities throughout the day to begin reaping the advantages of mindfulness.  
•    Be Mindful: It's simple to lose yourself in a hurry and have a blurry day. Take a moment to 
regain your breath. Try to stop and take in your surroundings. Make use of your five senses: 
taste, smell, touch, hearing, and sight. When you eat, for instance, take your time, smell your 
food, taste its taste, and feel its texture. Enjoy the occasion to the fullest and pay attention. 
•    Be Present in The Moment: Take a deep breath, observe your surroundings, and live in the 
present. Try to focus on your surroundings, your perceptions, and your physical sensations. 
Just observe; don't pass judgment as good or bad.  
Structured Mindfulness Exercises 
           You might want to try more structured mindfulness exercises if you feel that practicing 
mindfulness is enjoyable. Find a peaceful, cozy area to do this. Make sure you have enough time to 
give the exercises your whole attention. They don't have to take long, but you must have enough time 
to concentrate. A few instances of organized mindfulness exercises are as follows: 
1.   Mindfulness Breathing: 
        This is one of the most popular and straightforward ways to begin practicing mindfulness. Close 
your eyes and take a comfortable seat in a quiet area. Alternatively, if it's more comfortable, leave 
them open. Inhale slowly, deeply, and deeply through your nostrils. Exhale through your mouth. 
Then do this numerous times. Pay attention to your breathing. Be mindful of and in the present.  
2.   Mindfulness Body Scan: 
         This popular mindfulness practice helps you become aware of your body's emotions. A mindful 
body scan can reduce stress and promote relaxation. Additionally, it can promote body respect and 
awareness. 
Take a comfortable seat or lie down. If you are lying down, place your arms at your sides, palms 
facing up, and extend your legs while lying on your back. If you're seated, sit up straight. Inhale 
slowly and deeply through your nose, then exhale through your mouth. Continue taking deep breaths. 
Gradually shift your focus to each area of your body. Work your way down to your toes from your 
head. Alternatively, begin with your toes and work your way up to your head. Take note of any

feel or think about each bodily area. Refrain from criticizing your thoughts and emotions. 
3.   Mindful walking:  
         Take your time walking and pay attention to how each step feels. Observe how your feet move 
and maintain your body's balance. Pay attention to your surroundings and your senses, including 
what you see, hear, and feel.  
4.   Guided Imagery Technique 
     The mind is an effective instrument for healing. For ages, imagery (visualization) has been 
used in a variety of therapies to harness the power of the mind. You can lessen pain and other 
symptoms associated with your condition by conjuring up mental images. The visualization 
will probably be more beneficial if it is more detailed. In order to generate a form of fantasy 
that "removes" or gives a person a power over their current situation, people are instructed to 
envision sights, sounds, scents, tastes, or other sensations. Two imaging approaches are 
commonly employed:  
Palming: This calls for the visualization of color. You cover your eyes with your palms and 
visualize the stressful color that comes to mind. Typically, this is red. Next, you switch to a more 
calming hue like blue. It is believed that altering the hues in your imagination promotes relaxation.  
Guided imagery: Most frequently, guided imagery is employed as a method of relaxation. It 
entails visualizing yourself at a favorite serene location, such as beach, meadow, or forest, while 
peacefully sitting or lying down. This entails providing instructional advice that stimulates 
behavioral and physiological reactions as well as sensory experiences.  
To try guided imagery, one can do the following: 
1.   Take a comfy seat or lie down. 
2.    Shut your eyes. 
3.   To help the body begin to relax, take a few deep breaths. 
4.    Imagine a serene, tranquil environment, such a garden, a lake, or a place by the sea. 
5.   Imagine the setting and include further information. Examine the sight using all of your 
senses. For instance, picture the sound of birds chirping, the aroma of flowers, and the 
warmth of the sun. 
6.   Spend a few minutes in the scene, attempting to appreciate the serenity and sense of 
relaxation. One may decide to remain in the setting for as long as necessary to refresh 
themselves.  
7.    To return to the present, open your eyes and gently stretch. It is important for people to keep 
in mind that they are always welcome to return to the scene.

Benefits 
•    Reduces The Reaction to Stress  
     The body's fight-or-flight response becomes hyperactive when anxiety surges. You can 
slow down and concentrate on sensory input by using this strategy. Your nervous system 
receives a message of safety from this sensory interaction. Low heart rate, calm breathing, 
and a decrease in the surge of stress chemicals can all result from activating the 
parasympathetic nervous system.  
•    Cutting down on mental noise  
       The brain's default mode network (DMN), which is linked to overthinking and self-
referential thought, is shown to be less active during grounding exercises. Rather, your brain 
focuses on monitoring the surroundings, which inevitably softens recurrent negative thoughts. 
•    Enhances The Control of Emotions  
This exercise teaches you to stop, notice, and label your experience at each stage. This 
eventually creates a habit of reacting to stress consciously as opposed to instinctively. 
•    Increasing Presence and Concentration  
The activity brings you into the present moment, whether it's before bed, during a hectic 
workday, or even during a panic attack. You feel more steady, centered, and in charge after 
that change.  
•    Re-Anchoring Right Away in Times of Extreme Discomfort  
This method's structured sensory countdown helps your brain quickly stop panic episodes or 
traumatic flashbacks by bringing your attention back to the present. 
•    Neural Pathways Are Activated  
The brain's neural pathways are activated when you mindfully observe and describe what you 
perceive. This action increases awareness and improves focus. 
•    Breaks the Cycle of Anxiety  
You may swiftly divert your attention from bothersome thoughts by using your senses. You 
can stop worrying thoughts and turn your attention outside of yourself when you actively 
interact with the outside world.  
•    Calms the Body  
The parasympathetic nervous system can be triggered by concentrating on sensory 
information. This causes the body to go into relaxation mode, which lessens anxiety feelings.  
 
Grounding Techniques

It can be very challenging to remain in the "moment" when our minds are racing with different 
worries and anxious thoughts. However, the mindfulness tool has the ability to draw anxious mental 
energy back into the body, grounding and calming it so that you can relax and concentrate once more 
in the "here" and "now." During a panic attack, it works well to reduce anxiety. By shifting emphasis 
from thoughts, memories, or anxieties to the present, this method helps manage trauma based 
symptoms. 
 
Grounding techniques help the person to distract oneself from stressful triggers and memory-based 
triggers that increase anxiety, fears, and activate trauma-based memory. These keep the person to 
ground in the present moment. 
Activating Senses Through 5-4-3-2-1  
Activate your five senses by using 5-4-3-2-1 in the environment you are in: 
1.   Pinpoint and verbalise 5 things you see (like book, pen, table, chair, etc.). These could 
be a plant in the house or an image on the wall. Consider the hues and forms of what 
you are observing. What appeals to you about it? What aspects of it do you find 
objectionable? 
2.   Touch 4 things. This might be the chair you're in or the way your clothes feel against 
your skin. Consider how they feel: are they hard or soft? Rough or smooth? Warm or 
cold?  
3.   List hearing 3 sounds (like fan sound, bird chirping, sound of refrigerator, etc.), You 
might be able to hear the wind blowing or people outside. Consider the volume of the 
sounds. Do you hear these sounds frequently?  
4.   Take note of 2 things that you can smell (like any food item, perfume you are 
wearing, etc.). This could be the scent of your clothes, a flower, or the shampoo in 
your hair. Consider what appeals to you about that scent. 
5.   Taste 1 thing (like water, paper, sugar/salt, etc.). You can opt to taste your favorite 
food if you are unable to taste anything.  
Counting 
Distraction through grounding can be done through forward or backward counting. Like 
counting forward adding three each time like 3, 9, 12, 15, .... Or backward counting subtracting 3 
from 100 onwards like 97, 94, 91, .... This helps in distracting attention from anxiety provoking 
situation and get relaxed in panic situation. 
Pinpoint Colours 
Grounding can be done through concentrating in environment and pinpoint and name all 
objects in specific colour like all green things, yellow things etc. This helps to distract person from 
immediate stress and control anxiety and panic.
